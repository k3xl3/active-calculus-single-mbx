Item 3.2.3.4.b.

We can write \(q(x)\) as \(q(x) = \frac{1}{(x-c)e^x}\) and thus see the denominator increases without bound as \(x \to \infty\text{.}\) Therefore, \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{e^{-x}}{x-c} = 0\text{.}\) This makes the line \(y=0\) a horizontal asymptote for \(q\text{.}\) As \(x \to -\infty\text{,}\) \(e^{-x}\) increases without bound, while \((x-c)\) decreases without bound. Thus, \(q\) has an indeterminate form as \(x \to -infty\text{.}\) Applying L'Hopital's Rule gives us

\begin{equation*} \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^{-x}}{x-c} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{-e^{-x}}{1} = -\infty\text{.} \end{equation*}
